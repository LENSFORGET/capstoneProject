Abstract
Some of the biggest difficulties faced by inexperienced insurance consultants working in Hong Kong include ineffective client sourcing and product information overload. This capstone project aimed to tackle these problems by developing an AI co-pilot of the insurance consultancy powered by the NeMo Agent Toolkit created by NVIDIA. The lead generation function used to scrape user data with insurable intent off the platform Xiaohongshu. However, anti-scrapping restrictions, unstable logging functionality, and ethical concerns rendered this part of the project unusable, providing an important takeaway regarding real-world limitations on project feasibility. Thus, the main output of this capstone is the Intelligent Consultation Support System. It uses a Milvus vector database containing 32 Manulife Insurance Hong Kong documents to provide disclaimer-aware responses through the web application and an automated email extension. The implemented consultation path is based on a single NAT tool-calling orchestrator, FastMCP protocol, and category-aware retrieval-augmented generation, while specialist-agent containers are retained as extension components rather than the primary runtime logic. The overall assessment, which considered retrieval efficiency, generation accuracy, agent trajectory, and resilience, gave the system a baseline score of 0.812 in an ablation analysis study. The results suggest that category filtering and recovery capabilities are central to proper operation, while reranking-related analysis should be interpreted as an experimental evaluation dimension rather than a fully deployed production module. While some minor stability errors and generic answers suggest that this system cannot be deployed just yet, this end-to-end AI consultation prototype is still a meaningful outcome of the project. It can serve as a basis for future multi-agent-ready consultation, voice interaction, and ethically governed lead-intent research, provided that data access and compliance constraints are properly addressed.

Introduction
In the Hong Kong insurance market, contrasts are evident: plentiful opportunities meet plentiful obstacles. For beginner insurance agents, there are primarily two key issues to address. First is lead generation. Social-media marketing efforts, where one posts content on various channels, e.g., on Xiaohongshu, are likely to deliver very poor ROI on investment. The agent may devote a great deal of time to developing content; but the result will not be leads. It may even come as a feeling that content is blasted at the void without receiving engagement. Thus, a new insurance advisor will find themselves trapped in a “content creation pit”.
Secondly, there is the problem of knowledge requirements. Insurance products in Hong Kong are highly complex, voluminous, and regulated heavily. Premium structures, exemptions, riders, calculations for saving plans, medical underwriting guidelines, definition of critical illness—dozens of plans available per each carrier have all those nuances differentiating from one another. To the novice advisor, unprepared and lacking experience, this may turn out to be overwhelming. In a situation where the client poses some question regarding a concrete plan, the advisor simply won’t be able to quote the exact wording in the manual and thus will either provide an incorrect or ill-timed answer that could cost them the sale. That is why the “knowledge complexity issue” impedes the success of insurance selling.
The current research was originally conceived as a solution to both problems at once. Namely, the aim was to build an AI co-pilot prototype for a novice insurance agent, making use of NVIDIA's NeMo Agent Toolkit (NAT) and a multi-agent-ready architecture. In terms of architecture, the proposed system included two main functional branches:
An exploratory lead-generation branch through proactive harvesting of human-like online conversation from social media, powered by artificial intelligence and RPA, scoring the conversation for hidden insurance need, such as the need to apply for an international school or consider the best overseas investments.
Providing instant consulting support with answers based on intelligent searching through product documentations using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and the NAT-based ecosystem, mainly through agent-based tool invocation in the implemented prototype.
Thus, in the initial design, both modules were expected to form an assistive cockpit for novice advisors. In the final implementation, however, the lead-generation branch remained an exploratory and abandoned subsystem, while the consultation support branch became the main deliverable.
This paper progresses from foundations through methodology to implementation and conclusion. Chapter 2 covers the literature review in three key areas: foundation models architecture, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, arguing that AI systems should go beyond their generic nature to deliver domain-specific performance in the high-barrier environment of Hong Kong. Chapter 3 describes the methodology and design of the system, including the microservice-inspired layered design and embedding and retrieval strategies. Chapter 4 elaborates on design and implementation aspects of the Lead Generation module, detailing its functionality and exploring trade-offs involved in its development. Chapter 5 presents in detail the design and engineering of the Intelligent Consulting Support branch. Chapter 6 performs an in-depth multi-dimensional analysis of the architecture design using ablations and evaluating system failure modes. Lastly, Chapter 7 reviews progress, assesses system maturity and identifies future directions for continued development. These include building a genuinely collaborative multi-agent consultation system, incorporating voice interactions, and revisiting lead-intent research only under more stable and compliant data-access conditions.
At the beginning of the project, the goal was clear: creating more than just a simple conversational chatbot, but building a functional prototype of a domain-specific AI insurance assistant and, in the process, gaining first-hand experience with engineering an advanced AI system.

Literature Review
In response to the problems mentioned in the Introduction—the inefficiency of client acquisition and the difficulties experienced by novices when evaluating products for compliance in the Hong Kong insurance market—this study argues that general AI tools alone are insufficient for addressing the needs of high-barrier industries. Over the past decade, significant advances have been made in methodology, changing how machines handle text, speech, and high-dimensional data. Such developments have laid the theoretical foundation for overcoming the insurance industry's "content creation trap" and "knowledge complexity barrier." Below, the main literature related to this topic is reviewed, covering core model architectures, pre-training, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), parameter-efficient fine-tuning, and vector search technology.
2.1 Foundation Architectures and the Evolution of Pre-training
Foundations for modern language models were laid by early research in deep learning. Sutskever, Vinyals and Le (2014) proved that sequence-to-sequence models powered by LSTM networks could handle machine translation tasks extremely well, surpassing conventional phrase-based systems in translating English to French. The LSTM architecture itself solved the vanishing gradient problem, making it possible for models to learn dependencies lasting more than 1,000 time steps (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997).
After that, the pre-training paradigm became increasingly prominent in the field. Radford and Narasimhan (2018) claimed that unsupervised generative pre-training followed by task-specific fine-tuning could lead to breakthrough progress on numerous benchmarks for natural language understanding (NLU). Then, the GPT-2 model showed impressive zero-shot multi-task abilities after being trained on the WebText dataset (Radford et al., 2019). In parallel, BERT offered a new deep bidirectional Transformer architecture and set new performance records on 11 NLP tasks (Devlin et al., 2019).
Furthermore, regarding word representations, subword-based vector approaches model words as sets of character n-grams. It not only considerably improves the handling of rare words but also provides good support for out-of-vocabulary words (Bojanowski et al., 2016).
2.2 Reasoning Abilities and In-Context Learning
When model scales continued increasing, researchers started searching for ways to harness the latent reasoning abilities of large-scale models. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, where a model is encouraged to produce reasoning steps in between its answers, greatly improved results on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning tasks. For example, Wei et al. (2022) showed that with CoT prompting, the accuracy of a 175B-parameter GPT model on the GSM8K dataset jumped from 17.9% to 56.9%.
In addition to text-only tasks, in-context learning abilities have been expanded to the speech domain. Speech-augmented language models, such as SALM, consisting of a frozen text large language model (LLM) and audio encoders, adapters, and LoRA layers, could learn ASR and speech translation tasks in zero-shot mode, achieving performance comparable to task-specific fine-tuned Conformer models (Chen et al., 2023). Regarding speech recognition, Whisper-style models trained on 680,000 hours of web audio transcription data demonstrated robust zero-shot ASR performance, reaching human recognition accuracy without task-specific fine-tuning (Radford et al., 2022).
2.3 Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Infrastructure Support
In order to overcome the deficiencies of parametric models in terms of hallucination in knowledge-intensive tasks, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) techniques became crucial for dealing with fine-grained product clauses and regulatory requirements in Hong Kong insurance. Lewis et al. (2020) suggested combining a seq2seq model with a dense Wikipedia index, producing more specific, diverse, and factual outputs compared to purely parametric models. Building upon this approach, the Atlas model (Izacard et al., 2022) illustrated that a properly crafted few-shot RAG technique could achieve 42% accuracy on the Natural Questions dataset with only 64 examples, outperforming a 540B-parameter model, which was 50 times larger. Furthermore, Asai et al. (2023) proposed the Self-RAG technique that incorporated self-reflection and adaptive retrieval mechanisms, beating ChatGPT and LLaMA-2-based retrieval-augmented models in open-domain question answering, reasoning, and fact-verification tasks, greatly improving factuality and citation accuracy in long-form generation.
The ability of large-scale retrieval systems to function relies on robust vector search infrastructure. On the level of systems, the Milvus vector database has proven essential for handling large-scale vectors used for search, question answering, and recommendation systems (Wang et al., 2021). To speed up large-scale retrieval, Johnson, Douze and Jégou (2017) proposed GPU-based similarity search techniques using product quantization and optimized k-selection algorithms, allowing billion-scale k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) search, up to 8.5 times faster than previous GPU techniques. Also, the HQANN algorithm accelerated hybrid (attribute plus vector) queries, achieving 99% recall@10 on the GLOVE-1.2M dataset in around 50 microseconds (Wu et al., 2022). Taken together, these infrastructural elements form a sound basis for large-scale retrieval, allowing fast searches on product repositories for use in this project.
2.4 Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning and Development Tools
Along with the increase in the sizes of foundation models, full fine-tuning has become impractical in both computational and memory aspects, leading researchers to parameter-efficient adaptation techniques. The LoRA technique proposed by Hu et al. (2021) froze the weights of the base model and trained small low-rank matrices, reducing trainable parameters by up to 10,000 times and GPU memory usage by about threefold while matching or exceeding the performance of full fine-tuning on models such as GPT-3.
As for engineering implementation and development tools, the NeMo framework provided reusable neural modules for ASR and NLP tasks with distributed and mixed-precision training capabilities, thus facilitating the development, training, and deployment of large models (Kuchaiev et al., 2019). Such frameworks facilitate the usage of the NVIDIA NeMo framework to build the multi-agent system envisioned in this project.
2.5 Conclusion and Outlook
Collectively, the literature indicates that the advanced abilities of modern language and speech systems are driven by synergies in many aspects: large-scale pre-training ensures language understanding; prompt engineering, including chain-of-thought, facilitates deeper reasoning; RAG compensates for knowledge gaps; and parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques, such as LoRA, eliminate barriers to application. Meanwhile, infrastructural support involving vector databases and GPU-based similarity search enables applying these massive models in real-world large-scale environments.
Such scholarly advances constitute a comprehensive suite for tackling the "professionalism gap" and "client acquisition dilemma" in the Hong Kong insurance market, as discussed in this study. According to the literature, techniques such as Self-RAG may help mitigate hallucination risks in insurance compliance judgments, while engineering practices based on the NeMo framework ensure industrial-grade system stability. Leveraging the scholarly foundation described above, the following chapters will elaborate on how these technologies are applied in the NAT (NeMo-Agent-Toolkit) architecture to develop a multi-agent "copilot" system that can efficiently identify potential clients via AI plus RPA and assist in professional advisory services via RAG.

Methodology
This section presents the methodology for developing the "Hong Kong Insurance Multi-Agent Customer Acquisition Assistant" project based on NVIDIA's NeMo Agent Toolkit. Within this architecture, the system was designed as a multi-agent-ready prototype combining retrieval augmented generation with tool-based orchestration. The original design covered the complete path from public traffic mining to professional insurance consultation, but the implemented and reliable deliverable is the consultation support branch. Using a microservice approach, the software architecture consists of two parallel components – exploratory proactive customer acquisition and consultation support – and encompasses four interrelated layers. The bottommost layer – the perception and collection layer – involves monitoring public-domain activity through simulated browser behavior. In the next level – the knowledge hub layer – the Milvus vector database stores information extracted from Hong Kong insurance policies using the vector embedding approach. In the decision orchestration layer, the main dispatcher loads the workflow configuration and invokes tools through the NAT ecosystem; specialist-agent containers remain available as extension components, but they are not the primary consultation path. Lastly, the interaction layer transforms the system output into graphical user interfaces (GUI), thus closing one business cycle.
In order to explore low-cost customer acquisition, the project attempted an automated lead-generation approach that integrated large language models with robotic process automation. Data gathering was designed around a containerized xhs-login service combined with noVNC remote-desktop and headless browser technologies. Keyword search and comment mining were intended to support intention recognition through the MiniMax-M2.1 model. Through prompt engineering, the system attempted to go beyond keywords and identify underlying intentions related to Hong Kong insurance from comments about practical life scenarios, e.g., "applications for international schools" or "assets allocation for overseas countries." However, as Chapter 4 explains, this branch remained exploratory because platform controls, unstable data access, and ethical concerns prevented it from becoming a reliable final deliverable.
As for consultation support, to combat the problem of large language model hallucinations and ensure professional correctness, this project builds a deep RAG pipeline tailored for the processing of complex documents. In this paper, the term "Hong Kong insurance" refers specifically to the Manulife Hong Kong insurance offerings included in the project knowledge base. In the process of knowledge base creation, the restrictive clauses, rate tables, and guidelines of the Hong Kong Insurance Authority issued by Manulife Hong Kong were segmented into tokens and encoded into 1024-dimensional vectors using the nvidia/nv-embedqa-e5-v5 model. The vectors are then securely stored in the insurance_docs collection in Milvus. During consultation, the implemented runtime primarily relies on semantic vector retrieval and category-aware filtering; reranking is treated in this report as an experimental evaluation dimension rather than the confirmed core runtime path. The main agent interacts with the Milvus retrieval service via the interface created in rag_mcp.py script and injects the most pertinent knowledge snippets into the generative model to improve professional accuracy.
The system adopts NAT-based tool orchestration as its basis. In the implemented consultation path, the dispatcher loads the workflow_orchestrator.yaml configuration and runs a single tool_calling_agent that invokes the RAG MCP service. The repository retains specialist-agent service definitions for life insurance, savings and annuity, medical/VHIS, and critical illness use cases, but these are better understood as extension components rather than agents operating simultaneously in the primary runtime. Internally, the model node decides whether and how to call retrieval tools, while the tool node communicates with MCP services through structured standard input and output transport.
Evaluation of such an intricate system requires adopting a comprehensive multi-dimensional framework. Retrieval accuracy – namely, the degree of overlap between retrieved RAG documents and true clauses – is measured to verify the quality of the retrieval results, while Cohen's Kappa coefficient is used to compare manually tagged Xiaohongshu samples against the output of the lead recognition model. Additionally, end-to-end latency from the beginning of user interaction to the completion of multi-turn agent reasoning is measured in order to optimize concurrency. Overall, by leveraging the NVIDIA NAT framework, Milvus vector database, Next.js frontend interface, and advanced large models such as Qwen3.5 Plus and MiniMax, this project developed an AI-powered Hong Kong insurance automation prototype.
The Proactive Lead Generation Subsystem
4.1 Introduction
In reaction to the shift of the project scope from mere knowledge services to actionable business insights, the idea of designing a dedicated Proactive Lead Generation subsystem arose to detect hidden insurance needs in social media forums such as Xiaohongshu (RED). Considering the strong information asymmetry typical of the insurance industry, the module was meant to leverage intent recognition capabilities of large language models to analyze the semi-structured discussions related to life stages, health hazards, and financial planning. Designed as an exploratory feature, the subsystem used the NAT and MCP toolkit tools, browser automation, and relational databases to independently detect potential leads. Despite its demonstrable feasibility from the technical point of view, it faced several issues related to poor data acquisition, platform-specific risk controls, system stability, and ethical compliance, leading to the decision of excluding it from the final deliverables. As a result, the design was recognized as a knowingly abandoned but insightful research project.
4.2 Data Acquisition and Data Object Definitions
Given that social media such as Xiaohongshu provide pre-decision advisory intents in their image-text posts and user comments, it became the chosen platform. With no official API, the data acquisition process entailed the use of scripted Chromium instances for browser automation implemented with Docker in combination with noVNC or Windows. Initially, the relational database schema covered the posts, users, comments, and collection sessions; later on, it included AI-scrapped leads and interaction history (liked_posts) for closed-loop acquisition. Practically speaking, even though the persistence of posts and users went smoothly, commenting was highly unreliable, with the result of acquiring zero valid leads from comments in the leads table.
4.3 Experiment with OpenClaw
Early exploratory tests leveraged a general-purpose browser agent (OpenClaw) which supports web searches and screen-based data extraction with PowerShell-enforced strict behavioral boundaries and timeouts. What should be emphasized is the crucial distinction between web automation and proactive lead generation since the latter implies independent, context-dependent judgments of whether or not to treat each piece of content (comment) as a genuine opportunity. Thus, the prompts specified the number of comments to check while excluding authors. However, these tests revealed the fragility of generic browser agents when facing advanced platform security measures as dynamic loading, UI overlays, etc., would get the agent caught in the loop of "trying" to perform actions, "failing" with it, "reassessing," and "retrying." This meant that a generic browser could help to collect data, but a specialized system had to be used.

4.4 Architectural Design and Tool Selection
The new architectural approach embraced the use of an agent, PostgreSQL, and external data collection. The whole workflow in NAT was coordinated by tool_calling_agent while leveraging a browser (Playwright) in FastMCP and database tools, thus forcing the former to interact with the latter exclusively through explicit tool invocations with set transaction bounds. Notably, the ReAct prompting framework with its susceptibility to hallucinations and JSON parsing problems was replaced by the native function call format aligned with OpenAI guidelines, increasing the stability of action executions. While the system offered two deployment options (Docker and local), their reproducibility rate was exceptionally low due to extremely high sensitivity to browser state management and platform security controls.

4.5 Workflow Design and Experience
The theoretical workflow was to follow the sequence of steps involving initiation of database session, searching for keywords relating to top-priority insurance intents, visiting detail pages, recognizing true leads by analyzing comments, persisting data, and wrapping up or rolling back. However, in practice, it did not work as intended because a considerable number of comments in Xiaohongshu was linked to extensive DOM structures, increasing the number of tokens needed for one snapshot well above 20,000 and inference latency in the process. In addition, once an operation failed, it triggered a "perform operation-fail-reassess-retry" loop that increased the size of the context even further. Moreover, the underlying Playwright layer proved to be problematic because of daemon blocking, premature snapshotting, and zombie processes, making it impossible to build a stable system.
4.6 Actual Data
Practically speaking, it is clear that the subsystem failed to produce enough data needed to meet core capability requirements, having saved only 149 posts, 89 users, and 6 comments (yielding zero leads). The main reason behind this is the fact that true lead intent manifests in comments while not being expressed in posts. Even though initial session logs showed that the agent was capable of correctly extracting and assessing insurance intent from a few acquired comments, further operations were hindered by platform's anti-scrapping methods. In other words, all attempts at visiting detail pages, finding comments, and logging leads were blocked. In terms of expanding to multiple platforms, more or less successful smoke tests indicated that anti-scrapping and login dependency represent universal obstacles.
4.7 Reasons for Abandonment
As shown above, there were several reasons for abandoning this feature. First, it faced serious issues with robust data extraction in terms of identifying genuine leads rather than content authors. Second, platform controls and login dependencies adversely affected reproducibility. Third, its engineering was relatively costly from both time and computational perspectives. Fourth, proactive lead generation raised a number of ethical concerns. Fifth, handling web scraping-related challenges diverged from the project goal of building a reliable Q&A architecture based on official data sources.
4.8 Chapter Summary
This chapter provided the overview of exploratory research conducted around a social media-based lead generation subsystem which was built with NAT and FastMCP. Although experimentation validated the theoretical possibility of utilizing LLMs to detect insurable intent in semi-structured web data, implementation was mostly restricted by platform-specific security measures, excessive contexts, and lack of reliable data collection. Overall, the current project can be regarded as a staged research into the possibilities of proactive AI data mining. As shown by the results, the decision to exclude it from deliverables was rational since it did not comply with core capabilities and ethical standards.
The Intelligent Consultation Support System
5.1 System Positioning and Design Objectives
The Intelligent Consultation Support System is the central functional module of this project. Unlike the Proactive Lead Generation subsystem discussed in Chapter 4, which attempted to identify potential customers from external social platforms, this system focuses on the consultation stage after an insurance-related question has been raised. Its purpose is to provide searchable, interpretable, and traceable answers regarding product terms, coverage, eligibility, exclusions, claims, and product differences.
The need for this system arises from the complexity of insurance consultation. Official product documents are usually lengthy, clause-based, and difficult for inexperienced users or novice agents to navigate. Important distinctions may appear in coverage periods, premium payment modes, disease definitions, benefit conditions, exclusions, and claim procedures. As a result, consultation is not merely a conversational task; it requires locating the correct evidence and explaining it in a form that can be understood by non-specialists.
Accordingly, the system is positioned as a retrieval and explanation support tool rather than a substitute for licensed insurance advisors. It follows three design objectives. First, users should be able to ask natural language questions without using fixed product codes or rigid keywords. Second, answers should be grounded in official Manulife Hong Kong product materials instead of relying on the general parametric memory of a Large Language Model (LLM). Third, responses should remain readable while preserving necessary disclaimers on premiums, returns, coverage responsibilities, and professional advice. To achieve these objectives, the system adopts Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), where relevant document fragments are retrieved before the LLM generates a grounded response.
5.2 Overall Architecture
The system separates front-end interaction, agent orchestration, MCP-based tool invocation, vector retrieval, and document ingestion. Users interact through the nat-ui chat interface, which forwards requests to the nat-orchestrator service through HTTP streaming or WebSocket communication. The orchestrator executes a tool_calling_agent workflow defined in workflow_orchestrator.yaml, allowing the LLM to invoke structured tools rather than depending on ReAct-style text parsing.
The retrieval capability is exposed through the Model Context Protocol (MCP). In the workflow configuration, an MCP client launches rag_mcp.py through stdio transport. This FastMCP-based process provides three tools: search_insurance, get_collection_stats, and list_insurance_categories. The primary tool, search_insurance, converts the user query into an embedding, searches the Milvus insurance_docs collection, applies category-aware filtering where appropriate, and returns evidence snippets to the orchestrator.
The runtime environment is managed by Docker Compose. The main services include nat-ui, nat-orchestrator, Milvus standalone with etcd and MinIO, and the FastAPI backend nat-api. Although specialist agent containers and an HTTP routing tool remain in the repository, the implemented consultation path uses a single orchestrator to identify intent, invoke retrieval, and generate answers. This choice reduces latency and avoids unnecessary orchestration complexity while preserving extensibility for future multi-agent development.

5.3 Knowledge Base Construction
The knowledge base is constructed from 32 official Manulife Hong Kong PDFs and contains approximately 2,438 semantic chunks. The ingestion pipeline is implemented in rag_ingest.py, which follows a dual extraction strategy. MinerU is used where available to extract structured elements such as headings, text blocks, tables, image captions, and formulas. When MinerU is unavailable or fails, pypdf provides a fallback path for plain-text extraction.
After extraction, documents are cleaned and converted into retrieval-friendly text. HTML tables are transformed into readable text through table_html_to_text, while images are represented through captions or annotations when available. This design balances structural richness with operational robustness, because the system can still ingest documents even when advanced parsing is incomplete.
Documents are then segmented into chunks of approximately 600 characters with an 80-character overlap. This configuration preserves semantic continuity across neighboring fragments while reducing the risk of mixing unrelated topics in a single retrieval unit. Table-related chunks may be slightly longer in order to maintain structural coherence.
Vectorization and insertion are handled by embed_and_insert. Text chunks are embedded using NVIDIA NIM nv-embedqa-e5-v5, producing 1024-dimensional vectors. Each vector is inserted into Milvus together with metadata such as text, title, source file, URL, ingestion timestamp, and content type. Failed embedding batches are logged and skipped, allowing ingestion to continue under partial API failure.
5.4 Online Consultation Workflow
The online workflow begins when a user submits a question through nat-ui. The frontend forwards the message, conversation context, and generation parameters to the orchestrator. The backend then manages reasoning, retrieval, tool execution, and response generation.
Inside the orchestrator, the tool_calling_agent operates through model nodes, conditional routing, and tool execution nodes. The system prompt instructs the LLM to determine whether retrieval is required and, if so, to identify the relevant insurance category: life insurance, savings and annuity, medical and VHIS, or critical illness and comprehensive protection. Comparative questions may trigger multiple retrieval calls, while ambiguous or conceptual questions may use full-collection retrieval.
When a retrieval call is made, the NAT framework captures the structured tool invocation and communicates with rag_mcp.py through the MCP client. The RAG server normalizes category aliases, expands the query with category-specific context, embeds the query using the same NVIDIA model, and searches Milvus. If a category is specified, CATEGORY_MAP is used to construct a source-based filter. If no relevant result is found under the category filter, the system falls back to a broader search.
Retrieved evidence is returned as numbered blocks containing title, source, URL, and text. These snippets are appended to the LLM context, and the model generates the final answer. The expected response style is Traditional Chinese with a Hong Kong-oriented tone. The answer should summarize the relevant product information, explain practical considerations, and remind users that final interpretation is subject to official product terms and licensed professional advice.
5.5 Retrieval Augmentation and Category-Aware Mechanism
Category-aware retrieval addresses a major difficulty in insurance-domain RAG: different product types often share similar vocabulary. Terms such as protection, premium, claim, benefit, and cash value may appear across life insurance, medical insurance, critical illness products, and savings plans. Pure vector similarity may therefore retrieve fragments that are linguistically similar but commercially irrelevant.
To reduce this problem, rag_mcp.py defines CATEGORY_MAP, which groups product sources into four categories according to filename keywords: life insurance, savings and annuity, medical and VHIS, and critical illness or comprehensive protection. At runtime, category awareness is applied in two ways. First, the query is expanded with a Chinese category label to strengthen semantic alignment. Second, the Milvus candidate set is restricted through a source filter so that irrelevant product files are excluded before ranking.
The mechanism remains intentionally lightweight. It does not require a separately trained classifier or manual chunk-level labels. Instead, it injects domain knowledge into retrieval through interpretable metadata constraints. This is suitable for the project because it improves retrieval precision while keeping the system transparent and maintainable.
5.6 Frontend and API Support
The nat-ui frontend provides the main consultation interface. It supports chat input, streaming response display, session persistence through localStorage, and knowledge base status visualization. HTTP streaming is used as the default response mode, while WebSocket support remains available for interactive communication. A proxy layer adapts frontend requests to backend service routes.
The FastAPI backend api.py supports knowledge base management. Its functions include collection listing, status checking, document CRUD operations, PDF upload, ingestion triggering, and batch summary generation. Long-running ingestion progress is returned through Server-Sent Events, which makes document processing more observable from the interface.
The backend also includes a backup /api/chat endpoint that directly combines Milvus retrieval with an alternative LLM pipeline. However, this route bypasses the NAT agent workflow, structured tool invocation, and category-aware retrieval mechanism. Therefore, the main implementation of the Intelligent Consultation Support System remains the nat-ui -> nat-orchestrator -> rag_mcp.py pipeline.

5.7 Model and Tool Invocation Design
The system uses two model types: an embedding model for semantic retrieval and an LLM for language understanding and answer generation. NVIDIA NIM nv-embedqa-e5-v5 is used consistently during both ingestion and online query phases, producing 1024-dimensional vectors. This consistency reduces embedding-space mismatch between stored document chunks and user queries.
The generation model is accessed through an OpenAI-compatible interface, allowing provider-backed LLMs such as qwen-plus or minimax-m2.1 to be integrated with limited architectural changes. This decoupling reduces dependence on a single provider and improves the adaptability of the system.
The choice of tool_calling_agent is central to the implementation. Compared with ReAct-style workflows, structured tool calling makes retrieval invocation explicit and machine-readable, reducing parsing uncertainty. The RAG logic is also isolated as an MCP server, enabling retrieval behavior to be developed, tested, and extended independently from the LLM orchestration layer. To prevent uncontrolled reasoning loops, the maximum number of agent iterations is set to 8.
5.8 Engineering Reliability Considerations
Although detailed evaluation is presented in Chapter 6, several reliability considerations are already embedded in the system design. First, retrieval grounding is enforced by the workflow: product-specific answers are expected to rely on snippets returned from the official knowledge base. Second, the category-aware filter reduces cross-product contamination, especially when similar insurance terms appear in different product families. Third, fallback retrieval prevents the system from failing completely when category filtering is too restrictive.
The ingestion pipeline also includes robustness mechanisms. It supports fallback parsing through pypdf, truncates text fields according to Milvus schema constraints, and logs failed embedding batches without terminating the entire ingestion process. These choices are not intended as full evaluation results, but as engineering safeguards that make the prototype usable for later systematic assessment.
5.9 Limitations and Risk Management
The system is limited by the coverage and freshness of the ingested PDFs. If official documents are missing, outdated, or revised after ingestion, the system cannot automatically guarantee updated answers. RAG also cannot ensure absolute correctness: retrieval may miss relevant clauses, and generation may over-summarize or misinterpret complex product terms.
For this reason, the system must avoid definitive conclusions regarding premiums, investment returns, underwriting decisions, claim eligibility, or personalized recommendations. Its answers should remain explanatory and evidence-based, with clear reminders that official product terms and licensed advisors remain authoritative.
Several technical paths coexist in the project. The primary consultation path is nat-ui -> nat-orchestrator -> rag_mcp.py, while the FastAPI /api/chat endpoint and specialist agent containers serve as backups or future extensions. Social media data from the lead generation subsystem is deliberately excluded from the consultation knowledge base to prevent unofficial information from contaminating product answers.
5.10 Chapter Summary
This chapter described the design and implementation of the Intelligent Consultation Support System. Built on 32 official Manulife Hong Kong PDFs, the system combines RAG, category-aware retrieval, MCP-based tool invocation, Milvus vector search, and NAT agent orchestration to support insurance consultation.
The main contribution of this chapter is not to prove final performance, but to explain how the system is engineered: how documents are ingested, how queries are routed, how evidence is retrieved, and how answers are generated under traceable constraints. The detailed evaluation of this architecture, including metrics, ablation studies, and failure analysis, is therefore reserved for Chapter 6.

System Development and Evaluation 
6.1 Introduction: The Evolution of LLM Evaluation
With the deployment of large language model-based applications into enterprise-grade use-cases, traditional performance metrics, such as F1 and RMSE, become insufficient for measuring the dynamic interplay of retrieval, generation, and agent reasoning. To ensure the reliability of an enterprise solution, this chapter proposes a multilayered evaluation framework specifically designed to measure the performance of RAG + Agent architecture. Switching from subjective assessments towards data-driven experimentation and thorough ablation studies provides a technically justified basis for assessing the prototype.

6.3 Data Quality and Annotation Methodology
Validating the performance requires reliable ground-truth data. The data is divided into Train/Dev/Test partitions at the ratio of 60%/20%/20%. For reproducibility purposes, a random seed 20260424 was utilized throughout the process.
The pilot dual-pass annotation check showed high internal consistency, with Cohen's kappa coefficients reaching 1.0000 for should_refuse and 0.9222 for risk_label, both exceeding 0.75, which is commonly treated as strong agreement. Ambiguous cases involving missing evidence or questionable risk labels were manually reviewed and reconciled before being used in the evaluation.
6.4 The Tri-Layer Evaluation Metric Framework
The evaluation process is split into three layers, enabling the isolation of potential system bottlenecks while ruling out the possibility of mistaking retrieval failures for reasoning failures:
RAG Retrieval: measures the search and ranking efficiency with Recall@k, Precision@k, nDCG@k, and MRR.
RAG Generation: assesses the factual integrity based on the faithfulness and relevancy of generated answers relative to retrieved evidence and evaluates the presence of hallucinations.
Agent Trajectory: examines the degree of system autonomy by measuring the task success rate, tool call accuracy, recovery success rate, and timeouts.
All metrics are macro-averaged into a single Overall Score metric.
6.5 Experimental Design and Ablation Studies
To determine the contribution of each architectural component to the solution, four ablations are compared to the Baseline:
A1 (no_experimental_rerank): evaluates whether removing an experimental post-retrieval ordering step introduces more context noise.
A2 (no_category_filter): determines whether the absence of metadata filters causes cross-policy hallucinations.
A3 (topk=3): proposes that reducing the number of retrieved snippets to three creates information bottlenecks, especially when working with multi-hop queries.
A4 (no_recovery): tests whether turning off recovery strategy increases the frequency of errors occurring during the utilization of complex tools.

Conclusion and Future Directions
The proposed project uses retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) along with agent-based tool usage in order to cope with the complexity and information overload typical of insurance consultations in Hong Kong. Processing a total of 32 Manulife product manuals in order to produce an end-to-end AI application covering all stages from data ingestion to vector grounding, multi-level evaluation, and finally, a working user interface allows establishing an unequivocal benchmark in the author's journey from statistical analysis to systems engineering in terms of AI solutions. As a proof of concept, it addresses one of the core challenges faced by new advisers – the anxiety resulting from dealing with the vast policy portfolio available. However, being a prototype, the current solution has obvious limitations which mark potential areas for development. The current prototype operates primarily as a single tool-calling orchestrator rather than a fully collaborative multi-agent system. Specialist-agent services for life, savings, medical, and critical illness domains remain useful architectural assets for future extension, but they should not be interpreted as the core deployed consultation path. Similarly, the lead-generation branch should be understood as an exploratory and abandoned research attempt rather than a production-ready capability.
Additionally, implementing a prototype on top of NeMo-Agent-Toolkit v1.4 in order to be compatible with current research entails additional technical debt. It follows that switching to the updated version v1.6 of NeMo with self-tuning abilities ... will become a natural step further. In addition to optimization, future work will likely entail the inclusion of multimodal input/output capabilities. Given the presence of multiple languages in the Hong Kong insurance consultation environment, introducing a voice input and output channel through the Whisper model appears particularly important, which would convert a conventional web application into a mobile "insurance copilot" tool.
Ultimately, at the moment, the developed application does not demonstrate enough stability to be launched on production level, as sometimes outputs appear quite generic. Experiments with automating Xiaohongshu lead generation also demonstrated fragility and potential ethical concerns related to browser automation. Hardening of the product and conducting penetration tests along with moving beyond conventional web application interfaces towards more robust CLI-based communication will be required in order to make it production-ready. This project was never meant to produce commercially available AI insurance copilot, but rather served as a valuable learning experience. It demonstrated the possibility of such a solution while explaining the crucial difference between invoking an inference endpoint and building a proper AI system. Although achieving the latter is still some ways away, the path to success seems clearly marked now.
